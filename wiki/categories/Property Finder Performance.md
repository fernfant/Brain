## Key Insights

- **The quarter divides into two sharply different regimes and any cross-period comparison must account for this**: January–February shows a business tracking well against targets in a bull market (+58% YoY DLD in January). March–April shows the same business operating in a demand shock with forecasts at 53–66%. Using January KPIs to evaluate Q2 performance — or using March as the new normal — would both be errors. The correct frame is: W10 was a structural break, and Q2 should be evaluated against post-break baselines.

- **New Projects is the most volatile and most resilient segment simultaneously**: It took the biggest single-week hit in the dataset (–42% WoW in W10), then recovered the fastest (+29% above target in W13). This V-shape recovery — absent from secondary market metrics — confirms that off-plan developer demand is demand-deferred, not demand-destroyed. The business should model New Projects recovery separately from overall commercial recovery.

- **Exclusive Listings underperformed its target every single week across 13 weeks**: The 23K target was never reached. The metric stagnated at 11.7K for three weeks in January, jumped to 16.1K after NB Seal approvals, then drifted back to 16K by month-end. The rebaselining in February was a methodological correction, not a supply improvement. This is the most consistent underperformance in the entire dataset and represents a structural competitive gap.

- **App performance metrics told a consistently more positive story than commercial or web metrics**: App installs beat targets by 50–78% across February, MAU hit 454K vs 432K target, and App SoS consistently ran above Web SoS. But this app-first strength coexisted with NPS at 3 and below-target Mobile INP for most of the quarter — suggesting high consumer acquisition with degrading retention quality. Growing installs on a poor product experience is a temporary state.

- **Data infrastructure was a recurring constraint on decision-making quality**: Kochava failures (W3, W10, W11), CHEQ delays, SEMrush inconsistencies (W4), and Kochava/Redshift/GA4 discrepancies (W11) meant that at least 5 of 13 WBR weeks had significant metric reliability concerns. In a business making weekly commercial decisions under geopolitical uncertainty, bad data is not a minor operational issue — it is a strategic liability.

- **The relief package strategy preserves relationships but defers the pricing conversation**: With 79% of clients in relief conversations by W13, nearly the entire client book has been offered pricing relief to prevent churn. This was the right crisis response. But every discounted client is now anchored to a lower price point. The eventual re-pricing moment — when hostilities reduce and relief packages expire — will be the real commercial test of Q2.
